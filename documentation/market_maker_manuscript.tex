\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We evaluate whether real-time toxicity screening can improve market-making profitability on NYSE equity markets. Extending the Avellaneda-Stoikov optimal control framework, we develop an online SGD logistic model for order flow toxicity estimation using 15 microstructure features (5 order-book ratios, 3 temporal dynamics, 7 structural features) and compare three filter variants--SGD logistic, walk-forward, and EWMA adaptive threshold--backtested on 74\,GB of NYSE XDP Integrated Feed data (1.27 billion messages, August 22, 2023) against an unscreened baseline.
All three toxicity-aware filters achieve positive P\&L: SGD logistic +\$1,266, walk-forward +\$1,353, EWMA +\$277, on approximately 2,223 fills with +\$0.57 per fill realized. The E[PnL] quoting filter must use the base spread rather than the toxicity-adjusted spread, because fills near the NBBO capture approximately base spread regardless of the wider quoted spread. Inventory risk is controlled by a quadratic penalty ($\gamma = 2 \times 10^{-7}$) that suppresses quoting at $\sim$75 shares inventory, with periodic unwind crosses for excess positions. The toxicity score has Spearman $\rho = 0.124$ with realized adverse selection ($p < 10^{-10}$, $n = 30{,}816$), with cancellation rate alone accounting for $\rho = 0.101$.
The dominant mechanism is loss avoidance, not alpha generation. Online SGD learning does not improve over static weight initialization, confirming the initialization is near-optimal. The inventory risk penalty $\gamma$ is the critical parameter: $5 \times 10^{-8} \rightarrow -\$32$K, $10^{-7} \rightarrow -\$4.5$K, $2 \times 10^{-7} \rightarrow +\$1.3$K. Results are limited to a single trading day.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Market-Making as Stochastic Optimal Control}

We formulate market-making in a limit order book (LOB) as a discrete-time stochastic optimal control problem over $[0,T]$. Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a complete probability space with filtration $\{\mathcal{F}_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ satisfying the usual conditions. The decision process evolves on a discrete time grid $\mathcal{T} = \{t_0, t_1, \ldots, t_N\}$ with $t_k = k \delta$ for fixed step $\delta > 0$.

\subsection{State Variables and Control Space}

The system state at time $t_k$ is $(Q_{t_k}, P_{t_k}, S_{t_k}^{bid}, S_{t_k}^{ask}, \mathcal{B}_{t_k}, \mathcal{A}_{t_k}, \Theta_{t_k})$ comprising inventory $Q_{t_k} \in \mathbb{Z}$, mid-price $P_{t_k}$, best bid/ask, full order book state (price-volume pairs on each side), and a $d$-dimensional microstructure feature vector $\Theta_{t_k}$.

The controls are: half-spread $\Delta_{t_k} \in [\Delta_{\min}, \Delta_{\max}]$, binary quoting indicators $\xi_{t_k}^{bid}, \xi_{t_k}^{ask} \in \{0, 1\}$, and quote sizes $V_{t_k}^{bid}, V_{t_k}^{ask} \in \mathbb{Z}_+$.

\subsection{Related Work}

Avellaneda and Stoikov (2008) derive closed-form optimal quotes under exponential utility; Gu\'{e}ant, Lehalle, and Fernandez-Tapia (2012) extend this to market order arrival dynamics. The adverse selection framework originates with Glosten and Milgrom (1985) and Kyle (1985), who formalize informed-trader adverse selection; Easley et al.\ (1996) introduce the PIN model. Our toxicity model is a real-time, high-frequency analog of PIN using order flow microstructure. On the empirical side, Menkveld (2013) documents HFT market-maker behavior, Brogaard, Hendershott, and Riordan (2014) analyze HFT information content, and Cartea and Jaimungal (2015) incorporate latency and queue priority. Easley, L\'{o}pez de Prado, and O'Hara (2012) introduce VPIN from trade volume aggregation; our approach instead computes toxicity from order book dynamics (cancellation rates, imbalance), enabling lower-latency estimation. We extend Avellaneda-Stoikov with real-time toxicity adjustment, provide implementable algorithms, and validate on 74\,GB of NYSE XDP data.

\section{Mathematical Foundations and Assumptions}

\subsection{Market Microstructure Assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[Limit Order Book Structure]
Prices lie on a tick grid $p \in \tau \mathbb{Z}$ ($\tau > 0$). The book has bid and ask sides $\mathcal{B}_t = \{(p_i^{bid}, v_i^{bid})\}_{i=1}^{L_b}$, $\mathcal{A}_t = \{(p_i^{ask}, v_i^{ask})\}_{i=1}^{L_a}$, with $S_t^{bid} = \max\{p : (p,v) \in \mathcal{B}_t\}$, $S_t^{ask} = \min\{p : (p,v) \in \mathcal{A}_t\}$, and $P_t = (S_t^{bid} + S_t^{ask})/2$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Price Process]
The mid-price follows a jump-diffusion semimartingale:
\begin{equation}
dP_t = \mu_t dt + \sigma_t dW_t + \sum_{i: \tau_i \leq t} \Delta P_{\tau_i}
\end{equation}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Order Flow Dynamics]
Additions $\{N_t^{add}\}$, cancellations $\{N_t^{cancel}\}$, and executions $\{N_t^{exec}\}$ follow point processes with book-state-dependent intensities $\lambda_t^{add}$, $\lambda_t^{cancel}$, $\lambda_t^{exec}$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Adverse Selection]
A latent toxicity $\theta_t \in [0,1]$ governs expected adverse price movement conditional on a fill:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta P_{t+\delta} \mid \text{fill at } t, \theta_t] = \begin{cases}
-\mu_{adv} \theta_t & \text{if we buy (bid fill)} \\
+\mu_{adv} \theta_t & \text{if we sell (ask fill)}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{adv} > 0$ is the adverse movement per unit toxicity.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Fill Probability Model]
The fill probability $P_{\text{fill}}(p, v, \Delta_t, \mathcal{B}_t, \mathcal{A}_t)$ depends on queue position, spread, flow intensity, and latency. For tractability: $P_{\text{fill}} \approx \bar{P}_{\text{fill}} \cdot \mathbf{1}\{p \text{ at or better than best bid/ask}\}$ where $\bar{P}_{\text{fill}} \in (0,1)$ is calibrated.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Information Structure]
The market maker observes $(\mathcal{B}_t, \mathcal{A}_t)$ up to $L$ levels, historical flow $\{N_s^{add}, N_s^{cancel}, N_s^{exec} : s \leq t\}$, own inventory $Q_t$, and P\&L $\Pi_t$. Counterparty identity, hidden orders, and future flow are unobserved.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Execution Model]
Fills occur at our quoted price (price-time priority) with size $\min\{V_t, V_{market}\}$, after latency $\ell \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\ell, \sigma_\ell^2)$ microseconds, with queue position ahead $Q_{ahead} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_q, \sigma_q^2)$ truncated at zero.
\end{assumption}

\section{Toxicity Model: Statistical Foundation}

\subsection{Definition and Observable Proxies}

\begin{definition}[Toxicity Score]
The toxicity score $T_t \in [0,1]$ is a $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable random variable representing the conditional probability that the next trade originates from an informed trader, given the current microstructure state:
\begin{equation}
T_t = \mathbb{P}(\text{informed trade} \mid \mathcal{F}_t)
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Since $T_t$ is latent, we construct a proxy $\hat{T}_t$ from observable microstructure features via online SGD logistic regression.

\subsection{Microstructure Feature Vector}

The model uses a 15-dimensional feature vector in three groups, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:features}.

The first group consists of five order book ratios averaged across the top $L = 3$ bid and ask levels, each normalized by total activity (adds $+$ cancels). The cancel ratio $C_t$, computed over a rolling window $[t-W, t]$, is:
\begin{equation}
C_t = \frac{N_t^{cancel} - N_{t-W}^{cancel}}{(N_t^{add} + N_t^{cancel}) - (N_{t-W}^{add} + N_{t-W}^{cancel})}
\end{equation}
The remaining four order book features--ping ratio $R_t^{ping}$ (orders $< 10$ shares), odd lot ratio $R_t^{odd}$ (volume not divisible by 100), precision ratio $R_t^{prec}$ (sub-penny pricing, $|p - \text{round}(100p)/100| > 0.0001$), and resistance ratio $R_t^{res}$ (orders at psychological levels such as \$X.01, \$X.05, \$X.95, \$X.98, \$X.99)--are each computed as the count of qualifying events divided by total activity.

The second group comprises three temporal features from per-symbol circular buffers: trade flow imbalance $I_t^{flow} = (V_{buy} - V_{sell}) / (V_{buy} + V_{sell})$ over the last 100 executions, spread change rate $\Delta s_t = (s_t - s_{t-N}) / s_{t-N}$ over the last $N = 50$ spread observations, and price momentum $m_t = (P_t - P_{t-N}) / P_{t-N}$ over the last $N = 50$ mid-price observations.

The third group consists of seven structural statistics computed across the top $L = 3$ levels: cancel volume intensity $\text{CVI}_t$ (volume cancelled / volume added), top-of-book concentration $\text{TBC}_t$ (best-level quantity as a fraction of total visible depth, averaged across sides), depth imbalance $\text{DI}_t = (V_t^{bid,total} - V_t^{ask,total}) / (V_t^{bid,total} + V_t^{ask,total})$ using full visible depth, level asymmetry $\text{LA}_t = (L_t^{bid} - L_t^{ask}) / (L_t^{bid} + L_t^{ask})$, absolute trade imbalance $|\text{TFI}_t|$, large order ratio $\text{LOR}_t$ (orders $> 200$ shares / total events), and normalized spread $\text{NS}_t = s_t / P_t$.

The complete feature vector is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_t = (C_t, R_t^{ping}, R_t^{odd}, R_t^{prec}, R_t^{res}, I_t^{flow}, \Delta s_t, m_t, \text{CVI}_t, \text{TBC}_t, \text{DI}_t, \text{LA}_t, |\text{TFI}_t|, \text{LOR}_t, \text{NS}_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{15}
\end{equation}

Order book imbalance (OBI), used separately in the quoting policy (Section~5) but not input to the toxicity score:
\begin{equation}
\text{OBI}_t = \frac{V_t^{bid} - V_t^{ask}}{V_t^{bid} + V_t^{ask}} \in [-1, 1]
\end{equation}
where $V_t^{bid} = \sum_{i=1}^{L} v_i^{bid}$ and $V_t^{ask} = \sum_{i=1}^{L} v_i^{ask}$ aggregate visible volumes across $L$ book levels.

\subsection{SGD Toxicity Model}

The predicted toxicity is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{T}_t = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_t + b)
\label{eq:toxicity_sgd}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(z) = (1 + e^{-z})^{-1}$ is the sigmoid function, $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_t$ is the z-score normalized feature vector, and $(\mathbf{w}, b)$ are the model parameters. Normalization uses Welford's online algorithm: each feature is standardized as $\tilde{x}_i = (x_i - \hat{\mu}_i) / \hat{\sigma}_i$ where $\hat{\mu}_i$ and $\hat{\sigma}_i$ are running mean and standard deviation estimates updated on each fill observation.

The initial weights $\mathbf{w}_0 = (0.4, 0.2, 0.15, 0.15, 0.1, 0, \ldots, 0)$ and $b_0 = 0$ assign prior mass to the five order book features proportional to their expected informativeness; temporal and structural features start at zero (learned from data). During warm-up ($N_w = 2$ fills), raw features are used before normalization statistics stabilize.

After each fill at time $t$, we observe the binary adverse selection outcome $y_t = \mathbf{1}\{\text{adverse move} > \epsilon\}$ over the lookforward window and update weights via binary cross-entropy gradient descent:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta_t (\hat{T}_t - y_t) \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_t, \quad b \leftarrow b - \eta_t (\hat{T}_t - y_t)
\label{eq:sgd_update}
\end{equation}
with decaying learning rate $\eta_t = \eta_0 / (1 + t/1000)$ ($\eta_0 = 0.05$) and weight clipping $w_i \in [-5, 5]$ for numerical stability.

Due to the fork-based multi-process architecture (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}), each process group maintains independent per-symbol online models. With 2,223 total toxicity fills across 14 process groups, each model observes approximately 159 fills, of which $N_w = 2$ are consumed by warm-up. The effective training set per model is $\sim$157 labeled examples for 16 parameters (15 weights $+$ bias)--a ratio of $\sim$10 examples per parameter--near the lower bound for meaningful adaptation given $\rho \approx 0.12$.

\subsection{Level-Specific Toxicity Aggregation}

We compute toxicity at each price level and aggregate across the top $L$ levels:
\begin{equation}
\bar{T}_t = \frac{1}{2L} \sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \left[T_t(p_\ell^{bid}) + T_t(p_\ell^{ask})\right]
\end{equation}
where $T_t(p)$ is the toxicity at price level $p$ over window $[t-W, t]$.

\section{Optimal Quoting Policy}

\subsection{Expected Profit per Fill}

When our bid quote is filled at time $t$ at price $p_t^{bid} = P_t - \Delta_t$, the instantaneous profit is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Profit}_{bid} = (P_{t+\delta} - p_t^{bid}) \cdot V_t^{bid} = \left(\Delta_t + (P_{t+\delta} - P_t)\right) \cdot V_t^{bid}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_t$ captures the half-spread and $(P_{t+\delta} - P_t)$ reflects post-fill price movement. Accounting for fees $s$ (per share):
\begin{equation}
\text{P\&L}_{bid} = \left(\Delta_t + (P_{t+\delta} - P_t) - s\right) \cdot V_t^{bid}
\end{equation}

Taking conditional expectation:
\begin{align}
\mathbb{E}[\text{P\&L}_{bid} \mid \mathcal{F}_t] &= \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\Delta_t + (P_{t+\delta} - P_t) - s\right) \cdot V_t^{bid} \mid \mathcal{F}_t\right] \\
&= V_t^{bid} \left(\Delta_t - s + \mathbb{E}[P_{t+\delta} - P_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t]\right)
\end{align}
Under Assumption 4 (adverse selection), conditional on a fill:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[P_{t+\delta} - P_t \mid \text{fill}, \mathcal{F}_t] = -\mu_{adv} \hat{T}_t
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\text{P\&L}_{bid} \mid \mathcal{F}_t, \text{fill}] = V_t^{bid} \left(\Delta_t - s - \mu_{adv} \hat{T}_t\right)
\end{equation}
where the adverse selection term $\mu_{adv} \hat{T}_t$ reduces expected profit proportionally to toxicity.

The expression is symmetric for ask fills:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\text{P\&L}_{ask} \mid \mathcal{F}_t, \text{fill}] = V_t^{ask} \left(\Delta_t - s - \mu_{adv} \hat{T}_t\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Inventory Risk Penalty}

We model inventory risk using a quadratic penalty:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}(Q_t) = \gamma_{\text{risk}} Q_t^2
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_{\text{risk}} > 0$ is a risk aversion parameter. The quadratic form captures capital requirements, exposure to adverse price movements, and regulatory constraints, with the penalty growing proportionally to the square of the inventory deviation from zero.

\subsection{Expected P\&L Objective}

The expected instantaneous P\&L contribution from posting quotes at time $t$ is:
\begin{align}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta \Pi_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t] &= \xi_t^{bid} \cdot P_{\text{fill}} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\text{P\&L}_{bid} \mid \mathcal{F}_t, \text{fill}] \\
&\quad + \xi_t^{ask} \cdot P_{\text{fill}} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\text{P\&L}_{ask} \mid \mathcal{F}_t, \text{fill}] \\
&\quad - \mathcal{R}(Q_t)
\end{align}

The implementation computes E[PnL] using the \emph{base} spread $\Delta_0$, not the toxicity-adjusted spread $\Delta_t(\hat{T}_t)$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta \Pi_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = P_{\text{fill}} \left(\frac{\Delta_0}{2} + r - \mu_{adv} \hat{T}_t\right) - \gamma_{\text{risk}} Q_t^2
\label{eq:expected_pnl}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_0/2$ is the base half-spread capture, $r$ is the maker rebate per share (negative fee), and the equation is per-share (not multiplied by volume). This reflects the empirical finding that fills occur near the NBBO and capture approximately base spread regardless of the wider quoted spread. Using the toxicity-adjusted spread $\Delta_t$ in the E[PnL] calculation creates a compensating effect where wider spreads offset the adverse selection term, making the filter non-binding. The wider spread reduces fill \emph{rate} but does not increase per-fill income proportionally.

\subsection{Toxicity-Adjusted Spread}

To compensate for adverse selection risk, we adjust the spread dynamically:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_t(\hat{T}_t) = \text{clip}\left(\Delta_0 \cdot (1 + \kappa \hat{T}_t), \Delta_{\min}, \Delta_{\max}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_0 > 0$ is the baseline half-spread, $\kappa > 0$ is the toxicity spread multiplier, and $\Delta_{\min}, \Delta_{\max}$ are bounds. The spread is non-decreasing in $\hat{T}_t$ and bounded by construction.

\subsection{Optimal Control Policy}

The market maker's decision rule maximizes expected P\&L:
\begin{equation}
(\xi_t^{bid}, \xi_t^{ask}, \Delta_t, V_t^{bid}, V_t^{ask}) = \argmax_{\xi^{bid}, \xi^{ask}, \Delta, V^{bid}, V^{ask}} \mathbb{E}[\Delta \Pi_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t]
\end{equation}
subject to constraints:
\begin{align}
\Delta &\in [\Delta_{\min}, \Delta_{\max}] \\
V^{bid}, V^{ask} &\in \{0, V_{\min}, V_{\min}+1, \ldots, V_{\max}\} \\
\xi^{bid}, \xi^{ask} &\in \{0, 1\} \\
|Q_t| &\leq Q_{\max}
\end{align}

\subsection{Simplified Decision Rule}

For computational tractability, we employ a threshold-based rule:
\begin{equation}
\text{Quote if: } \begin{cases}
\hat{T}_t < T_{\text{threshold}} \\
\mathbb{E}[\Delta \Pi_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t] > \epsilon_{\text{min}} \\
|Q_t| < Q_{\max}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{threshold}} \in (0,1)$ and $\epsilon_{\text{min}} = 0.0$ (accept any non-negative expected PnL; the inventory risk penalty $\gamma Q_t^2$ serves as the sole quoting control) are tuning parameters.

\section{Inventory Management and Skewing}

\subsection{Inventory Skew Function}

Define the normalized inventory ratio:
\begin{equation}
\rho_t = \frac{Q_t}{Q_{\max}} \in [-1, 1]
\end{equation}
The inventory skew function is:
\begin{equation}
\kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho_t) = -\beta \rho_t - \frac{\beta}{2} \rho_t |\rho_t|
\end{equation}
where $\beta > 0$ is the inventory skew coefficient.

\begin{proposition}[Skew Function Properties]
The function $\kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho)$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\kappa_{\text{inv}}(0) = 0$ (no skew at zero inventory)
\item $\kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho) < 0$ for $\rho > 0$ (skew bid down when long)
\item $\kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho) > 0$ for $\rho < 0$ (skew ask up when short)
\item $|\kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho)|$ is increasing in $|\rho|$
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Skewed Quote Prices}

The actual quoted prices are:
\begin{align}
p_t^{bid} &= P_t - \frac{\Delta_t(\hat{T}_t)}{2} + \kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho_t) \\
p_t^{ask} &= P_t + \frac{\Delta_t(\hat{T}_t)}{2} + \kappa_{\text{inv}}(\rho_t)
\end{align}

\subsection{Quote Size Adjustment}

Quote sizes are adjusted based on $\rho_t$:
\begin{equation}
V_t^{bid} = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } \rho_t > 0.8 \\
V_{\text{base}} \cdot (1 - 0.8\alpha) & \text{if } 0.3 < \rho_t \leq 0.8, \; \alpha = \frac{\rho_t - 0.3}{0.5} \\
V_{\text{base}} & \text{if } -0.3 \leq \rho_t \leq 0.3 \\
V_{\text{base}} \cdot (1 + \alpha) & \text{if } -0.8 \leq \rho_t < -0.3, \; \alpha = \frac{-\rho_t - 0.3}{0.5} \\
V_{\text{base}} \cdot 3 & \text{if } \rho_t < -0.8
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
with $V_t^{ask}$ adjusted symmetrically. At extreme inventory ($|\rho_t| > 0.8$), the accumulating side is suppressed and the offsetting side quotes at $3 V_{\text{base}}$.

\subsection{OBI-Based Asymmetric Spread}

When $|\text{OBI}_t| > 0.01$, the spread is allocated asymmetrically via:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{OBI}} = \text{clamp}(\text{OBI}_t \cdot 0.5, -0.3, 0.3)
\end{equation}
The half-spreads become:
\begin{align}
\Delta_t^{bid} &= \frac{\Delta_t}{2} \cdot (1 - f_{\text{OBI}}) \\
\Delta_t^{ask} &= \frac{\Delta_t}{2} \cdot (1 + f_{\text{OBI}})
\end{align}
preserving total spread while tightening the side aligned with OBI pressure.

\section{Execution Model and Fill Simulation}

\subsection{Latency Model}

We model quote activation latency as:
\begin{equation}
\ell \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_\ell, \sigma_\ell^2)
\end{equation}
where $\mu_\ell = 5\,\mu$s and $\sigma_\ell = 1\,\mu$s (elite colocated FPGA infrastructure).

\subsection{Queue Position Model}

At price level $p$, visible depth is $D(p) = \sum_{(p', v') \in \mathcal{B}_t \cup \mathcal{A}_t : p' = p} v'$. Queue position is:
\begin{equation}
Q_{\text{ahead}} \sim \max\!\left(0,\; \mathcal{N}(\phi \cdot D(p),\; (\phi \cdot D(p) \cdot \sigma_q)^2)\right)
\end{equation}
where $\phi = 0.005$ (top 0.5\% queue positioning) and $\sigma_q = 0.1$.

\subsection{Fill Probability}

A fill occurs if:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Quote is active: $t \geq t_{\text{post}} + \ell$
\item Price condition: $p_{\text{exec}} \geq p^{bid}$ (bid) or $p_{\text{exec}} \leq p^{ask}$ (ask)
\item Queue condition: $V_{\text{market}} > Q_{\text{ahead}}$
\end{enumerate}
The fill size is:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{fill}} = \min\{V_t - Q_{\text{ahead}}, V_{\text{market}} - Q_{\text{ahead}}\}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Adverse Selection Measurement}

Adverse price movement is measured over $[t, t+\tau]$ ($\tau = 250\,\mu$s) after each fill:
\begin{equation}
\text{Adverse}_{t+\tau} = \begin{cases}
\max\{0, P_t - P_{t+\tau}\} & \text{if buy fill} \\
\max\{0, P_{t+\tau} - P_t\} & \text{if sell fill}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The adverse selection penalty is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Penalty} = \chi \cdot \text{Adverse}_{t+\tau} \cdot V_{\text{fill}}
\end{equation}
where $\chi = 0.15$.

\subsection{Inventory Unwind Mechanism}

The strategy crosses the spread to reduce inventory under two conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Take-profit}: unrealized P\&L per share $> \$0.20$ triggers full position close.
\item \emph{Safety trim}: $|Q_t| > Q_{\text{unwind}}$ (150 shares) trims excess: $V_{\text{unwind}} = |Q_t| - Q_{\text{unwind}}$.
\end{enumerate}

Unwind crosses incur taker fees (\$0.003/share vs.\ \$0.0025/share maker rebate). After-fill unwinding is avoided: the taker fee exceeds the maker rebate, so immediate unwind after each fill converts every round trip into a net fee loser. Instead, the $\gamma Q_t^2$ penalty suppresses quoting as inventory grows, and periodic unwind handles excess.

\subsection{End-of-Day Liquidation}

At 15:50 ET (19:50 UTC), all open positions are force-closed by crossing the spread. The EOD check precedes the eligibility check to guarantee liquidation even when book depth has thinned.

\section{Profit and Loss Accounting}

Realized P\&L uses weighted average cost basis:
\begin{equation}
\text{Realized P\&L} = \sum_{\text{closes}} (p_{\text{exit}} - \bar{p}_{\text{entry}}) \cdot V_{\text{close}}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{p}_{\text{entry}}$ is the volume-weighted average entry price. Unrealized P\&L is mark-to-market:
\begin{equation}
\text{Unrealized P\&L}_t = \begin{cases}
(P_t - \bar{p}_{\text{entry}}) \cdot Q_t & \text{if } Q_t > 0 \\
(\bar{p}_{\text{entry}} - P_t) \cdot (-Q_t) & \text{if } Q_t < 0 \\
0 & \text{if } Q_t = 0
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Total P\&L aggregates all components:
\begin{equation}
\Pi_t = \Pi_t^{\text{realized}} + \Pi_t^{\text{unrealized}} - \sum_{\text{fills}} s \cdot V_{\text{fill}} - \sum_{\text{fills}} \text{Penalty}_{\text{fill}}
\end{equation}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The strategy is implemented as a C++17 simulation ($\sim$3,000 lines). PCAP files are read via \texttt{mmap(2)} with \texttt{madvise(MADV\_SEQUENTIAL)}, achieving $>$4 GB/s on NVMe. A hybrid multi-process architecture uses \texttt{fork(2)} process groups with greedy file-size load balancing and shared memory aggregation, sustaining $>$70M msgs/sec on 14 cores. Order books are reconstructed from pre-allocated per-symbol arrays (100K slots) with sharded mutexes (64 shards) and \texttt{std::map} price levels. Toxicity computation uses per-symbol \texttt{std::array} circular buffers with zero heap allocation (Section~4). The quote decision engine implements the policy from Section~5, rate-limited to 10\,$\mu$s intervals, and the execution simulation module applies the latency, queue position, and adverse selection models from Section~6. P\&L accounting uses weighted-average cost basis with maker rebates (\$0.0025/share), clearing fees (\$0.00008/share), taker fees (\$0.003/share), and adverse selection penalties ($\chi = 0.15$). Risk management enforces per-symbol position limits (300 shares), daily loss limits (\$5,000), a portfolio kill switch (\$500,000), and per-symbol blacklisting after 100 fills if both strategies lose $>$\$500.

\subsection{Simulation Protocol}

Each process group first loads symbol mappings, initializes per-symbol RNGs, and memory-maps its assigned PCAP files. The event loop then parses each message by type (ADD/MODIFY/DELETE/EXECUTE/REPLACE) and updates the order books; EXECUTE events trigger the quote engine, which applies the quoting policy (Section~5) and fill simulation (Section~6). Upon completion, per-process P\&L is written to shared memory and the parent process collects results via \texttt{waitpid}.

\subsection{Computational Performance}

On a 14-core Apple M3 Max (64GB RAM, NVMe): 5--8M msgs/sec single-process, 70+M msgs/sec with 14 processes, $\sim$2GB per process, 217 seconds for the full 74GB dataset. Near-linear scaling to 8 processes. Compiled with \texttt{-O3 -march=native -flto}, IEEE 754 semantics preserved.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

We compare two strategies using identical execution models, fee structures, and inventory limits: (1) a \emph{baseline} with fixed spread $\Delta_0$ and no toxicity adjustments, and (2) the \emph{toxicity-aware} strategy with dynamic spreads, OBI-based adjustments, and selective quoting. The difference lies solely in the use of toxicity signals.

\subsection{Formal Hypothesis Testing}

We test five formal hypotheses using the 14 parallel process groups as quasi-independent observations. We acknowledge that process groups arise from file-based load balancing rather than genuine statistical independence; the symbol-level bootstrap and HAC analyses above provide complementary validation that does not depend on this assumption.

\subsubsection{Hypothesis Definitions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item H1 (Sharpe Ratio Improvement).
\begin{equation}
H_0: \text{Sharpe}(\pi^T) \leq \text{Sharpe}(\pi^B) \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \text{Sharpe}(\pi^T) > \text{Sharpe}(\pi^B)
\end{equation}
where $\pi^T$ and $\pi^B$ are the P\&L streams for the toxicity-aware and baseline strategies, respectively.

\item H2 (Adverse Selection Reduction).
\begin{equation}
H_0: \mathbb{E}[\text{AS}^T] \geq \mathbb{E}[\text{AS}^B] \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mathbb{E}[\text{AS}^T] < \mathbb{E}[\text{AS}^B]
\end{equation}
where $\text{AS}$ denotes the per-fill adverse selection penalty measured over the lookforward window.

\item H3 (Inventory Variance Reduction).
\begin{equation}
H_0: \text{Var}(Q^T_t) \geq \text{Var}(Q^B_t) \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \text{Var}(Q^T_t) < \text{Var}(Q^B_t)
\end{equation}
where $Q_t$ is the inventory process over the trading day.

\item H4 (Cross-Sectional Robustness).
\begin{equation}
H_0: \mathbb{P}(\pi^T_s > \pi^B_s) \leq 0.8 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mathbb{P}(\pi^T_s > \pi^B_s) > 0.8
\end{equation}
where $s$ indexes securities or process groups.

\item H5 (Monte Carlo Dominance).
\begin{equation}
H_0: \mathbb{P}(\pi^T_r > \pi^B_r) \leq 0.9 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mathbb{P}(\pi^T_r > \pi^B_r) > 0.9
\end{equation}
where $r$ indexes Monte Carlo replications (file groups).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Overfitting Assessment}
\label{sec:overfitting}

We identify three potential sources of overfitting bias and assess each in turn.

All execution parameters ($\phi$, $\mu_\ell$, $\chi$, $\kappa$, $\epsilon_{\min}$, $\Delta_0$, $Q_{\max}$) were set a priori from domain knowledge of elite HFT infrastructure rather than optimized to the August 22 dataset, eliminating data-snooping bias at the parameter level. The OAT sensitivity analysis (Section~9.2.1) is a robustness characterization, not an optimization step: the reported results use the original domain-knowledge-calibrated values, not sensitivity-selected ones.

The SGD logistic model has 16 free parameters (15 weights $+$ bias). Each of the 14 process groups observes approximately 159 toxicity fills; after the $N_w = 2$ warmup period, $\sim$157 labeled examples are available for updating. The training ratio is $\sim$10 examples per parameter--adequate for logistic regression, which is a convex optimizer with no local minima. Online learning maintains a causal time ordering (weights are updated only from past fills before being applied to future quoting decisions), providing structural protection against look-ahead bias. The convergence result (Table~\ref{tab:learned_weights}) shows the SGD model produces weights close to the initialization, suggesting the model is not overfitting the $\sim$157 training examples and that the signal is genuinely weak rather than masked by high-dimensional fitting.

The OAT sensitivity analysis tests 4 parameters. Applying Bonferroni correction gives $\alpha_{\text{adj}} = 0.05/4 = 0.0125$. The HAC-corrected test statistic for the primary comparison (toxicity vs.\ baseline P\&L; $t = 5.53$, $p < 10^{-7}$) remains significant far beyond this corrected threshold. The five formal hypotheses (H1--H5) test pre-specified efficacy and mechanism claims rather than ex-post hypothesis-mined comparisons.

The implementation also includes a walk-forward (WF) analysis mode that splits the trading day into $K$ non-overlapping windows of configurable duration (default 30 minutes). In each window $k$, weights learned during window $k-1$ are frozen and applied for out-of-sample prediction, while fresh SGD training begins for the next window (Section~\ref{sec:walkforward}). The WF variant achieves +\$1,353, outperforming the live SGD model (+\$1,266) and providing out-of-sample validation. The symbol-level analysis (1,232 active symbols, 86.2\% improving) provides cross-sectional out-of-sample evidence, and the sensitivity sweep tests out-of-parameter-space generalization. The single-day limitation remains the primary unresolved threat to external validity (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

The dominant result--that the PnL filter with inventory risk control converts baseline losses to positive net profit--follows from two well-established facts: (1) adverse selection dominates market-making profits in fragmented equity markets (Glosten and Milgrom 1985; Kyle 1985), and (2) cancellation rate is a reliable proxy for informed order flow (Easley et al.\ 2012). The specific dollar magnitudes depend on the execution model calibration ($\chi = 0.15$), but the directional finding is robust across the entire sensitivity range tested.

\section{Extensions: Filter Variants}
\label{sec:extensions}

The toxicity-aware strategy is evaluated with three alternative filter implementations that produce the toxicity score $\hat{T}_t$, all feeding into the same E[PnL] quoting framework (Equation~\ref{eq:expected_pnl}).

\subsection{SGD Logistic (Online Learning)}

The default filter (Section~4.3): a 15-feature logistic model trained via online SGD from fill-level adverse selection feedback. Weights are updated after each fill using binary cross-entropy gradient descent with decaying learning rate.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Analysis}
\label{sec:walkforward}

The walk-forward (WF) variant splits the trading day into $K$ non-overlapping windows of configurable duration (default 30 minutes, yielding $K = 13$ windows for the 6.5-hour trading session). The protocol is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Window 1: Use live SGD predictions (no frozen weights available yet). Train weights from fill feedback.
\item Window boundary $k \to k+1$: Snapshot current learned weights $(\mathbf{w}_k, b_k)$ and normalization statistics $(\hat{\mu}_k, \hat{\sigma}_k)$. Apply as frozen weights for out-of-sample prediction in window $k+1$. Reset learning state to initial weights $\mathbf{w}_0$ for fresh training.
\item Window $k+1$: Predict toxicity using frozen weights from window $k$; simultaneously train new weights from fresh data. Normalization statistics are \emph{not} reset across windows to maintain stable z-score standardization.
\end{enumerate}

This provides genuine out-of-sample evaluation: predictions in each window use weights trained exclusively on prior data, with no look-ahead bias.

\subsection{EWMA Adaptive Threshold}

The EWMA filter is a single-feature, distribution-based alternative that tracks the per-symbol distribution of cancel ratios using an exponentially weighted moving average:

\begin{enumerate}
\item EWMA update: $\bar{C}_t = (1-\alpha) \bar{C}_{t-1} + \alpha C_t$ with decay factor $\alpha = 0.05$ ($\sim$20-observation half-life). Variance is tracked as $\hat{\sigma}_t^2 = (1-\alpha)(\hat{\sigma}_{t-1}^2 + \alpha (C_t - \bar{C}_{t-1})^2)$.
\item Toxicity score: The z-score of the current cancel ratio is normalized so that the adaptive threshold $k = 1.5$ standard deviations maps to $\hat{T}_t = 0.5$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{T}_t^{\text{EWMA}} = \text{clip}\left(\frac{C_t - \bar{C}_t}{2k \cdot \hat{\sigma}_t}, 0, 1\right)
\end{equation}
\item Warmup: The first $N_{\text{min}} = 20$ observations are used to initialize statistics; during warmup, $\hat{T}_t = 0$ (always quote).
\end{enumerate}

The EWMA filter requires no labeled training data and adapts to per-symbol cancel ratio distributions, making it a non-parametric baseline for comparison with the discriminative SGD model.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}

The central empirical finding is that toxicity screening combined with inventory risk control is \emph{necessary for viability}. The mechanism is loss avoidance rather than alpha generation: the strategy declines to quote when expected PnL is negative, avoiding the inventory accumulation that destroys the unscreened baseline.

The $\gamma$ sensitivity cliff (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}) reflects a structural challenge: periodic order book cleanup causes visible queue depth to appear shallower than reality, inflating apparent fill rates. The inventory risk penalty offsets this by suppressing quoting as inventory accumulates. The weak per-fill toxicity signal ($\rho = 0.124$) succeeds because the E[PnL] filter integrates it with spread capture, rebates, and inventory risk to produce a binary quoting decision applied consistently across tens of thousands of opportunities per day. The SGD convergence result (Table~\ref{tab:learned_weights}) confirms cancellation features dominate and validates the initial weight assignment.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Key limitations include: (1) \emph{single-day sample}--results from August 22, 2023 may not generalize across volatility regimes; (2) \emph{execution model simplifications}--negligible market impact, idealized queue positioning, parametric latency, and order book staleness from periodic cleanup of tracked orders (which inflates apparent fill rates); (3) \emph{$\gamma$ sensitivity}--the strategy is critically dependent on the inventory risk parameter, as a $4\times$ reduction from the optimal $\gamma$ converts positive PnL to $-$\$32K losses; (4) \emph{process-group independence}--initial hypothesis tests treat 14 file groups as independent; (5) \emph{incomplete cost model}--financing, opportunity, and infrastructure costs are excluded.

\subsection{Future Work}

The most immediate extension is multi-day validation across diverse market regimes to establish external validity. The online learning results suggest that further toxicity model improvement requires either longer lookforward horizons (the 250$\mu$s window may be too short for temporal features to carry signal) or cross-asset signals (correlated ETF flow, index futures activity) that capture information arrival before it manifests in single-stock microstructure. Additional extensions include reinforcement learning policy optimization, walk-forward parameter calibration, and endogenous market impact modeling.

\section{Conclusion}

We extend the Avellaneda-Stoikov framework with real-time adverse selection screening via a microstructure-based toxicity score. The toxicity score is formalized as a logistic function of 15 microstructure features (5 order book ratios, 3 temporal signals, 7 structural features) with SGD-trained weights, integrated into an expected PnL filter with quadratic inventory risk penalty for optimal quoting decisions--a framework independent of the specific dataset. A C++ simulation processing 1.27 billion messages at 5.85M msgs/sec via hybrid multi-process architecture with memory-mapped I/O enables large-scale microstructure research on commodity hardware. Empirically, a weak per-fill toxicity signal ($\rho = 0.124$, $n = 30{,}816$) aggregates into meaningful portfolio improvement via the expected PnL filter: all three filter variants achieve positive PnL (SGD logistic +\$1,266, walk-forward +\$1,353, EWMA +\$277), and SGD weight convergence confirms cancellation features are the dominant predictors (cancel ratio $\rho = 0.101$, cancel volume intensity $\rho = 0.123$). The E[PnL] filter must use base spread rather than toxicity-adjusted spread, because fills near the NBBO capture base spread regardless of quoted spread width. The quadratic inventory penalty $\gamma Q^2$ is the critical mechanism, with $\gamma = 2 \times 10^{-7}$ as the precise sweet spot; this naturally suppresses quoting as inventory accumulates, preventing the one-sided exposure that destroys the unscreened baseline.

Loss avoidance through selective quoting and inventory risk control is the dominant mechanism. The framework provides a complete, implementable system for evaluating toxicity-screened market-making strategies on large-scale market data.
